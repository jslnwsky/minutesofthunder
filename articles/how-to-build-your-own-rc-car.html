How To Build Your Own RC Car: A Modern Guide to Kit Building

DIY Guide • 10 minute read • Updated for modern RC technology

There's something deeply satisfying about building an RC car from a kit. Sure, you can buy an RTR (ready-to-run) model, rip open the box, and be driving in minutes. But when you build it yourself, you understand every bolt, every gear mesh, every linkage. That knowledge pays dividends the first time something breaks at the track — and trust me, something always breaks at the track. Here's how to get started with kit building.

Why Build Instead of Buying RTR?

RTR cars are convenient, no question. But building a kit gives you advantages that an RTR simply can't:

  • You learn the car inside out. When a ball joint pops or a turnbuckle bends, you'll know exactly what it is and how to fix it because you're the one who put it there.
  • Better build quality. Factory-assembled RTR vehicles sometimes have loose screws, poorly shimmed diffs, or threadlock where it shouldn't be. When you build it, every screw gets the right torque.
  • Component choice. Most kits are sold without electronics, which means you pick your own motor, ESC, servo, and radio. You're not stuck with whatever budget electronics the manufacturer bundled in.
  • It's genuinely enjoyable. If you like working with your hands, a kit build is a great way to spend a few evenings. The manuals are typically excellent, with detailed exploded diagrams for every subassembly.

Types of RC Kits

Not all kits are created equal. Here's what you'll find on the market:

Full Kit (Unassembled)

Everything you need to build the rolling chassis — frame, suspension, drivetrain, body, hardware. You supply the motor, ESC, servo, radio, battery, and charger. This is the traditional kit experience and gives you the most control. Brands like Tamiya, Yokomo, and Team Associated are well known for quality competition kits.

ARR / ARF (Almost Ready to Run)

The chassis comes pre-assembled, but you still need to add electronics (motor, ESC, servo, radio) and a battery. This is a middle ground — you skip the mechanical build but still choose your own electronics. Good option if you want component choice without the full assembly time.

RTR with Kit Mentality

Some experienced hobbyists buy an RTR and immediately disassemble it, re-grease the diffs, re-shim the bearings, and rebuild it to their spec. It's more expensive than buying a kit, but some popular platforms (like the Traxxas Slash or Arrma Typhon) don't have true kit versions, so this is the workaround.

Tools You'll Need

You don't need a full workshop, but you do need the right tools. Cheap tools strip fasteners and cost you more in the long run. Here's the essential list:

Must-Have Tools

  • • Hex driver set (1.5mm, 2.0mm, 2.5mm, 3.0mm)
  • • Phillips screwdriver (small and medium)
  • • Needle-nose pliers
  • • Flush cutters (for trimming body posts, zip ties)
  • • Hobby knife (X-Acto or similar)
  • • CA glue (thin and medium viscosity)
  • • Blue threadlock (Loctite 242 or equivalent)

Nice to Have

  • • Nut driver set (4mm, 5.5mm, 7mm, 8mm)
  • • Shock pliers (for adjusting spring preload)
  • • Soldering iron (for motor and battery connectors)
  • • Calipers or ruler for measuring ride height
  • • Small parts tray or magnetic mat
  • • Body reamer (for enlarging body post holes)
  • • Diff grease and bearing oil

Pro Tip

Invest in quality hex drivers from the start. MIP, Dynamite, and Hudy all make RC-specific sets that fit metric hardware precisely. A stripped 2.5mm hex screw in a steering knuckle at the track is not how you want to spend your afternoon.

Choosing Your First Kit

For a first build, consider these factors:

  • 1/10 scale is the sweet spot. It's the most popular size for racing and bashing. Parts availability is excellent, and there are tons of upgrade options.
  • Match the kit to how you'll drive. If you want to bash in the backyard, a buggy or short course truck kit makes sense. If you want to race on-road, look at touring car kits. For crawling, there are dedicated crawler kits from brands like Vanquish and Element RC.
  • Check parts availability. Before buying a kit, make sure replacement parts are readily available. Mainstream brands like Tamiya, Team Associated, TLR (Team Losi Racing), Yokomo, and XRAY have strong parts support. An obscure kit might be fun to build but frustrating when you snap an A-arm and can't find a replacement.
  • Read the manual first. Most manufacturers publish their kit manuals online as PDFs. Download it before you buy. If the instructions are clear and detailed, you'll have a much better build experience.

Assembly Tips That Actually Matter

I've built more kits than I can count, and these are the things that make the difference between a car that drives well and one that fights you:

  • Read each step completely before you start it. Don't just glance at the diagram and start grabbing parts. Read the text, look at the diagram, identify all the parts, then assemble.
  • Don't over-tighten screws into plastic. RC cars use a lot of self-tapping screws into plastic parts. Tighten until snug — if you feel the screw start to spin freely, you've stripped the hole. CA glue and a re-drive can sometimes save it, but prevention is better.
  • Build the shocks carefully. Shocks are the most precision-dependent part of the build. Fill them slowly to minimize air bubbles, bleed them properly, and make sure the o-rings are lubricated. Good shocks make a huge difference in how the car handles.
  • Use threadlock on metal-to-metal fasteners. Any screw going into a metal nut or insert should get a small drop of blue threadlock. Vibration will loosen untreated screws faster than you'd expect.
  • Check gear mesh. Whether it's the spur gear and pinion or the diff gears, proper mesh is critical. Too tight and it'll bind and wear prematurely. Too loose and you'll strip teeth. The classic test: slip a piece of paper between the gears, tighten until the paper drags slightly, then remove the paper.

Adding Electronics

Once the chassis is built, you need to install the electronics. For a typical kit build, you'll need:

Required Electronics

  • • Motor — Brushless is the modern standard. Match the KV to your application.
  • • ESC — Must be compatible with your motor type and cell count.
  • • Servo — Steering servo. Look for adequate torque and speed for your vehicle weight.
  • • Radio system — A 2.4GHz transmitter and receiver. Even budget 2.4GHz systems are reliable.

Power

  • • Battery — LiPo is standard. 2S (7.4V) for most applications, 3S for more power. Make sure it fits your car's battery tray.
  • • Charger — A balance charger capable of charging your battery chemistry. Dual-port chargers are handy for charging two packs.
  • • Connectors — Match your battery connectors to your ESC. Deans, XT60, and EC3 are common. Soldering may be required.

Many hobby shops sell motor/ESC combo packages that are pre-matched, which takes the guesswork out. For a first kit build, a sensored brushless combo in the 3000-4000KV range paired with a 2S LiPo is a solid, versatile setup.

Your First Run

Before you send it full throttle, do these checks:

  • Check steering direction and trim. Turn the wheel on the transmitter — does the car steer the correct direction? Adjust your servo reversing and trim before driving.
  • Check throttle direction. Make sure forward is forward and brake is brake. Sounds obvious, but it's an easy mix-up.
  • Wheels on the ground test. Set the car on the ground and give it gentle throttle. Does it pull to one side? Check your steering trim.
  • Listen for binding. Lift the car and spin each wheel by hand. They should spin freely. If you hear grinding or feel resistance, something's too tight.
  • Start slow. Do a few laps at half throttle. Check that nothing is coming loose, nothing is rubbing, and the car tracks straight. Then gradually increase speed.

After your first session, go over the car again. Check for loose screws, look for wear marks that indicate rubbing, and make sure the gear mesh hasn't shifted. A post-run inspection is a good habit to develop from day one.

Beyond the Kit

Once you've built your first kit, the real fun starts. You'll find yourself wanting to optimize — stiffer sway bars for less body roll, aluminum shock towers for durability, a different pinion gear for more top speed. This is the upgrade path, and it's where the kit-building knowledge really pays off. You already know exactly how the car goes together, so swapping parts is straightforward.

Building an RC car from a kit isn't just a cheaper way to get into the hobby (though it can be). It's a fundamentally different experience that connects you to the machine in a way that tearing open an RTR box never will. Give it a shot — you might find that the building is half the fun.
